After hearing and reading about the recent developments involving GameStop and Wall Street, I’ve learned a lot about not only how the stock market and its primary shareholders operate, but how those operations can be upended – and how that upending fits into a broader societal context.

These are the themes that I've encountered as an information scientist that resonate with the current situation and stakeholders' responses to it:

  • Ethical use of data
  • Human-computer interaction (HCI) in app design
  • Data privacy

Ethical data usage

One issue that I’ve been learning about is the ethics of using posts from sites like Twitter and Reddit as sources and citing them in articles – how do you navigate discussing issues that are so heavily online while still protecting the users’ information? Is it ethical to use these posts in articles and studies, even though they’re already public and could technically be used by anyone? What are the ethical implications of exposing certain users and potentially putting them on the spot via their posts – could that lead to harassment or worse? These are all important questions to consider when reporting on an event like this one, where so much of the activity revolves around online participation and coordination.

HCI in app design

In terms of how users have been able to invest individually, the financial apps in use have also received a fair amount of attention, including suggestions for implementing requirements for financial apps so users fully understand the risks of investment. Robinhood, a commission-free stock trading and investing app, uses a gamified interface to make investing seem easier than it is and encourage users to trade frequently, which most personal finance experts don’t recommend for long-term investing. Without properly understanding the risk, many consumers are thus steered into investment decisions that are ultimately costly. While many could argue that users should be doing that research on their own before investing on the apps, some have claimed that using information design to obfuscate the real-world implications of users’ actions on these apps is often actively harmful.

Data privacy

Amidst this market volatility, regulators are looking into how the rally started and whether new rules should be created because of it. This has the potential to create similar ethical dilemmas to the ones mentioned previously – should regulators be able to monitor online discussion forums like r/WallStreetBets when tracking down the offending investors and enforcing new market regulations, if such rules are to come about? While such monitoring to prevent similar incidents and avoid further market instability is one potential solution, it seems a bit like a violation of Internet privacy – and the backlash from users who would perceive this as unwarranted surveillance would almost certainly be a roadblock.

Conclusion

While the GameStop-Wall Street fiasco may seem like an isolated incident, especially for those of us who don’t have a stake in the outcome and are thus less involved, it represents a sort of populist revolt following a similar pattern wherein internet-based insurgents have coordinated an establishment, with varying degrees of success. This type of coordinated behavior by investors – and the resulting chaos – has also caused issues to arise regarding enforcing securities law; while it may seem like a pump-and-dump scheme on the outset, the users aren’t lying about the stock or falsifying information – they appear to have gathered enough traders to buy up GameStop’s stock and push up the prices through legal means. The results of this saga still remain to be seen – but it’s clear that these events may have consequences beyond the original goals.
