Since I don’t watch very much television, I chose MLP:FiM as a topic for this essay because it’s the only show I’m familiar enough with at the moment to have a clear idea for a five-paragraph essay on the subject. My Little Pony: Friendship is Magic (MLP:FiM) is a flash-animated fantasy TV series based on the toys and animated works belonging to Hasbro, airing from October 10, 2010 to October 12, 2019. The show follows a unicorn named Twilight Sparkle as she learns about friendship with her five friends – Applejack, Rarity, Fluttershy, Rainbow Dash, and Pinkie Pie – in the town of Ponyville. I’m not familiar with a lot of the modern cartoons aimed at children, but this series stands out to me for several reasons. One prominent element of the show is its all-female main cast, which lends itself to the diverse personalities that challenge idealized stereotypes of what girls should be and thus ties into a key element of the show: that friends can be different, even getting into arguments, and still be friends. However, for the sake of this argument, I’ll focus on the amount of dialogue that various characters have as evidence of the show’s heavy focus on its female main characters and side characters and to explain how this differs from other, similar shows.


From the episode transcript data, we can gather that out of the main cast, Twilight Sparkle has the most lines in the series, followed by Rainbow Dash, Pinkie Pie, Rarity, Applejack, and Fluttershy, in that order. Twilight’s dragon assistant, Spike actually has more than Fluttershy but less than Applejack – although since he is a prominent character from the very first episode, it stands to reason that he would have more lines than the quiet, timid Fluttershy. Following the main cast, the next few characters with the most dialogue are also female; the Cutie Mark Crusaders (Apple Bloom, Scootaloo, and Sweetie Belle, respectively) and Starlight Glimmer have the next highest quantities of dialogue. At this point, there is a sharp drop in the number of lines each character has; Discord, the character with the next highest number of lines, has 746 (as compared to Sweetie Belle’s 1010). One aspect to note is that the transcripts contain a lot of separation in terms of character dialogue tags; young versions of characters are referred to differently than their older counterparts, two or more characters speaking or singing in unison have a combined dialogue tag, some animals and inanimate objects have dialogue at certain points in different episodes, and so on – while this data accurately reflects the amount of dialogue and interaction that characters in the show have, there may be some errors in the exact numbers.


Even without the dialogue tags, anyone who has a passing familiarity with the show can gather that the show has a heavy focus on female characters – the current rulers of Equestria are Princesses, as well as a few of the main villains, and many of the background characters are female as well. A modern show that like this one that features a prominently female cast of characters is certainly not unheard of, but stands out because of its entrance into popular culture – a fact that is due in no small part to the unlikely audience of male fans between the ages of 13 and 35 within the fandom that the series has amassed over the years, drawn to the show by a variety of factors that include diverse cast of characters – with depth that is, naturally, propelled by the dialogue between ponies and the interactions they have.

I want to acknowledge that this isn’t to disregard some of the FiM’s issues; the show has a problem with its depictions of racism, especially in later seasons, and some of the ‘friendship lessons’ touted at the end of each episode end up having the potential to give children the wrong impression about what they should do in certain situations (Newbie Dash and Parental Glideance come to mind), but I would argue that the show is generally a strong example of why girls’ cartoons can still have interesting characters with depth – and, as we can see from the diagrams above and the data from transcripts, the dialogue between characters in MLP:FiM arguably does this well for the main cast.
